## Introduction 

ACEs Medical System can be intimidating at first, but as a group we play with a relatively simple set of options that add enough depth whilst not being too intrusive for the average player.

Unlike the advanced options, our setup is designed to be quick and straightforward, you only need to focus on the following:

<ul>
  <li>Stopping Bleeding</li>
  <li>Managing Pain</li>
  <li>Regaining Consciousness</li>
  <li>Calling A Medic</li>
</ul>

This guide will walk you through exactly what to do to treat an injured teammate, the function of each medical item, and how to navigate the ACE Medical Menu. By the end, you'll be able to confidently patch up your squadmates and keep the fight going.


---

## Medical Items

In our system, items are simplified, so there's no worrying about wound types or advanced triage. Here's what each one does:

<ul>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Bandages</font></strong>Used to treat ANY wounds. Will prevent further bleeding.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Tourniquet(CAT)</font></strong>Instantly stops all bleeding on a limb. Prolonged use causes pain.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Morphine</font></strong>Greatly reduces pain and reduces BP.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Painkillers</font></strong>Moderately reduces pain. Has no effect on BP.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Epinephrine</font></strong>"Wake Up Drug" - revives unconscious but stable patients.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Blood IV</font></strong>Replenishes lost blood volume over time. Comes in 250mL, 500mL and 1000mL variants. Saline has an identical function.<strong><font color="red">Only usable by Medics.</font></strong></li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Splint</font></strong>Used to treat fractures. Will restore mobility upon use.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82FF9F">Personal Aid Kit</font></strong>Restores a stable casualty to full health, and removes all negative effects. <strong><font color="red">Only usable by Medics.</font></strong></li>
</ul>

## The Medical Menu

ACE uses a medical menu as the interface for diagnosing a patient and administering aid.

To access the menu, you can use the keybind ("H") OR you can use the ACE interaction menu/self-interaction menu.

![Medical Menu](https://cdn.fkcontent.eu/monthly_2021_12/medicnew1.png.3887b141f18c8168338776d1b29d66f7.png)

When you open medical menu you'll be greeted by name of the patient (1), a panel of buttons (2), a click-able figurine (3) and an overview of the patient (4).

The name of the patient in the top left is self-explanatory, but worth double-checking you are treating who you intend to treat.

The figurine is click-able, and you can switch between the six different parts of the body: Legs, Arms, Head and Torso. Once you've selected a body part, the Overview panel on the right side provides information about that body part.

The colour of the parts on the figurine also give you a quick idea of their state:

<ul>
  <li><strong>White</strong>: No wounds, bandaged or otherwise are present.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="#82E1FF">Blue</font></strong>: Wounds are bandaged or bruises are present.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="yellow">Yellow</font></strong> to <strong><font color="red">Red</font></strong>: Wounds are open, increasing in severity from yellow to red.</li>
</ul>

The panel of buttons mentioned previously are laid out like so:

![Medical Menu button panel](https://cdn.fkcontent.eu/monthly_2020_04/20200401125056_1.jpg.d6c9f7424f9d4c501b489f1136871f55.jpg)


<ul>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(1)</font></strong>Triage Card - A history of treatment</li>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(2)</font></strong>Diagnose - Checking pulse, blood pressure, responsiveness</li>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(3)</font></strong>Bandages & Fractures - Treating wounds</li>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(4)</font></strong>Medication - Intravenous drugs, morphine/epinephrine etc.</li>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(5)</font></strong>Advanced Treatment - PAKs and CPR (mainly for medics only)</li>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(6)</font></strong>Drag/Carry - Options for dragging or carrying the patient</li>
  <li><strong><font color="red">(7)</font></strong>Toggle Self - Switches between yourself and others</li>
</ul>

### Triage Card

The Triage Card simply shows a history of treatment. Not as important in our setup, but can be used to stop medics from double-dosing and ODing the patient OR stop you from wasting precious medical resources.

On the image below, we can see a Tourniquet and a dose of Morphine and Ephinephrine have already been administered to this patient.

![triage card](https://cdn.fkcontent.eu/monthly_2020_04/20200428092616_1.jpg.7139ae2aa5f4227a6f96157da93e77ee.jpg)

### Diganose

The Diagnose tab allows you to check pulse and blood pressure. In our setup, this is rarely required, but checking BP/Heart Rate can sometimes help you decide if you need to do any CPR. Responsiveness will also allow you to tell the different between dead and alive patients.

### Bandages & Fractures

The Bandages & Fractures tab will allow you to apply bandages, splints and tourniquets to treat wounds.

![Bandages and fractures tab](https://cdn.fkcontent.eu/monthly_2021_12/medicnew2.png.73d179775f8ccd359f23d27c37ff821d.png)

You can treat a wound by selecting the injured body part, then select your desired treatment from the left-hand tab. 

In the image above, you can see how a Tourniquet has been applied to the Left Leg using the Bandages & Fractures menu. You may also notice that whilst the wound is still present, bleeding has stopped for now thanks to the Tourniquet.

We can then apply a Splint to fix the fracture:

![splint heal](https://cdn.fkcontent.eu/monthly_2021_12/medicnew3.png.ed09cb202b29c21bf1d2129922f2e8ae.png)

We can also apply a bandage to completely heal the wound - finishing our treatment of the limb!

![bandage heal](https://cdn.fkcontent.eu/monthly_2021_12/medicnew5.png.0a41369fa39184c16ca1a20fc5a8a012.png)

### Medication

To apply drugs, you will need to go to the Medication tab. You must then select a limb (arms or legs) and select your medication from the left-hand panel. Keep in mind that Tourniquets will prevent the flow of drugs from the affected limbs as long as they are applied, so be sure to remove them.

### Drag & Carry

Dragging and Carrying actions are available from the corresponding tab. These options can be useful for transporting unconscious/immobilised casualties.

Dragging is fast to activate, but slow and only offers imprecise movement.

Carrying is slow to activate, but allows fast movement when finished.

Picking between which option you need is something you'll learn by playing.

### Toggle Self/Others

Finally, this switch works fairly simply. Clicking it will toggle between the medical menu for yourself and whoever you're looking at within range.

## Treatment Procedure

So, you come across an injured squadmate, they are unconscious, what do you do?

Here is a rough flow of actions you can take to treat a casualty:

<ol>
  <li><strong>Secure The Area</strong> - There's not much point starting treatment if you are still exposed/taking direct fire. Your first priority should be to secure the immediate area. Focus on making it safe to begin treatment, and moving your patient to cover.</li>
  <li><strong>Check Status</strong> - Is the patient alive? Are they bleeding? How severe? Do they have any fractures? Figure this out before you go ahead and start treating.</li>
  <li><strong>Notify Team</strong> - Somebody should be covering your back whilst you administer aid - let them know where you are and what you are doing.</li>
  <li><strong>Call A Medic</strong> - If this squadmate is severely injured, enough that they are going to need blood and/or lots of treatment, get on your radio and call for a medic - or better yet, start shouting for one!</li>
  <li><strong>Start Treatment</strong> - Whilst you wait for a Medic, start fixing.
  <ol>
      <li>Your first priority should be preventing blood loss. Throw Tourniquets on limbs you cant treat for the moment.</li>
      <li>Start Bandaging other wounds, then circle back to the limbs.</li>
      <li>Remove Tourniquets to allow blood to flow.</li>
      <li>Now that the wounds have been dealt with, patch up any fractures with a Splint.</li>
      <li>Administer Morphine for the pain.</li>
      <li>Administer Epinephrine ONLY if the patient is stable and will wake up.</li>
    </ol>
  </li>
  <li><strong>Get To A Medic</strong> - If we assume the patient is no longer bleeding, your next focus should be getting them to a medic, who can give blood enough to allow them to wake up via Epinephrine.</li>
</ol>



### Epinephrine or Not Epinpehrine?

Whether or not you should give someone Epinephrine to wake up depends on the amount of blood they have lost.

If the patient has only Lost Some Blood, you are OK to administer Epinephrine.

If the patient has Lots A Lot of Blood or more, do not administer Epinephrine - they will not wake up from it.



### Why has someone fallen unconscious?

Falling unconscious can happen for several reasons, which are handy to know as you begin your treatment.

<ol>
  <li><strong>Pain</strong> - Too much pain can cause someone to fall unconscious at random moments.</li>
  <li><strong>Low Blood</strong> - If a patient suffers from excess blood loss, they may fall unconscious. This is serious, as only Medics have the ability to replenish blood.</li>
  <li><strong>Direct Trauma</strong> - A severe wound to a major organ can cause someone to go unconscious, even if the pain or blood loss hasnt hit that threshold yet.</li>
</ol>
